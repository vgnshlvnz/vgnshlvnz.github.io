Circuit Switched Calls

Question:

Explain circuit switched Calls

Answer:

A circuit-switched call is a type of telephone call that is established by setting up a dedicated connection, or circuit, between the two parties for the duration of the call. In a circuit-switched call, the connection is maintained until the call is completed and the circuit is released. In a circuit-switched network, such as the traditional public switched telephone network (PSTN), a connection is established between the two parties by routing the call through a series of switches and transmission lines. The connection remains in place for the duration of the call and is released when the call is completed. Circuit-switched calls are typically used for voice calls, but they can also be used for other types of data, such as fax or modems. Circuit-switched networks are generally reliable and provide high-quality voice calls, but they are not as efficient as packet-switched networks, as they require a dedicated circuit to be established for each call, even if the call is not using the full capacity of the circuit.

Explain packet switched Calls

A packet-switched call is a type of telephone call that is transmitted over a packet-switched network, such as the Internet. In a packet-switched call, the call data is divided into smaller units called packets, which are transmitted over the network and reassembled at the destination.

Packet-switched networks are more efficient than circuit-switched networks because they transmit data in packets that can be shared among multiple users. Packet-switched networks use a technique called multiplexing to share the available bandwidth among multiple users, which allows more efficient use of the network resources.

There are several types of packet-switched calls, including:

Voice over Internet Protocol (VoIP) calls: VoIP calls are telephone calls that are transmitted over the Internet using the Internet Protocol (IP). VoIP calls can be made using a VoIP phone, a computer with a VoIP client, or a smartphone with a VoIP app. 2. Video calls: Video calls are telephone calls that include video as well as audio. Video calls can be made using a video call service, such as Skype or Zoom, or using a smartphone with a video call app. 3. Instant messaging (IM) calls: IM calls are telephone calls that are transmitted over an IM network, such as WhatsApp or Facebook Messenger. IM calls can be made using a smartphone with an IM app or a computer with an IM client. Packet-switched calls are generally less expensive than circuit-switched calls because they use the Internet rather than the traditional telephone network. They also offer additional features, such as the ability to send text messages, share files, and make video calls. However, packet-switched calls may not have the same level of reliability and call quality as circuit-switched calls.

Question:

What is PTSN?

Answer:

PSTN stands for Public Switched Telephone Network. It is the traditional telephone network that is used for circuit-switched telephone calls. The PSTN is a global network of interconnected telephone lines, switches, and other equipment that is used to transmit telephone calls and other types of communication, such as fax and data.

The PSTN is a circuit-switched network, which means that a dedicated connection, or circuit, is established between the two parties for the duration of the call. The connection is maintained until the call is completed and the circuit is released.

The PSTN is operated by telecommunications companies, or carriers, which provide telephone services to customers. The PSTN is the primary means of telephone communication in many parts of the world, and it is used to transmit both voice and data calls.

However, the PSTN is being replaced in many areas by newer technologies, such as VoIP (Voice over Internet Protocol) and cellular networks, which offer additional features and more efficient use of network resources. Despite this, the PSTN is still in use in many parts of the world and is likely to continue to be used for some time to come.

The voice data transmitted during a VoLTE call is typically encoded using a codec (coder-decoder) that compresses the data to reduce the bandwidth required for the call. Common codecs used for VoLTE include Adaptive Multi-Rate (AMR) and Enhanced Voice Services (EVS).

The control data transmitted during a VoLTE call includes information about the call setup, maintenance, and termination, as well as other call-related information, such as the caller's identity and the call duration. The signaling data transmitted during a VoLTE call includes information about the call setup, maintenance, and termination, as well as other call-related information, such as the caller's identity and the call duration. The signaling data is typically transmitted using signaling protocols, such as the Session Initiation Protocol (SIP) or the ISDN User Part (ISUP).

Overall, the typical VoLTE call traffic includes the audio and other data transmitted between the two parties during the call, as well as the control and signaling data used to establish and maintain the call. VoLTE offers several benefits over traditional circuit-switched voice calls, including improved call quality, faster call setup, and additional features, such as the ability to send text messages and share files during a call.

Question:

Explain 4G

Answer:

4G, or fourth-generation, is a mobile communication standard that is designed to provide faster data transfer speeds and a more reliable connection than previous generations of cellular technology. It is a successor to 3G technology, which was the dominant mobile communication standard in the early 2000s.

4G networks use a variety of technologies and protocols to provide faster data speeds and a more reliable connection. Some of the key features of 4G include:

High data transfer speeds: 4G networks are designed to offer data transfer speeds of up to 100 megabits per second (Mbps) for mobile devices and up to 1 gigabit per second (Gbps) for fixed devices, such as routers. This is significantly faster than 3G networks, which typically offer data transfer speeds of up to 42 Mbps.

Improved coverage: 4G networks use a variety of technologies and protocols to improve coverage and reduce the number of dropped connections. This includes the use of advanced antenna technologies, such as MIMO (multiple input, multiple output) and beamforming, which can improve the range and reliability of the connection.

Enhanced voice quality: 4G networks use Voice over LTE (VoLTE) technology to provide high-quality voice calls. This technology uses the Internet Protocol (IP) to transmit voice data, which allows for higher-quality calls with less latency (delay) than traditional circuit-switched systems.

Increased capacity: 4G networks use advanced technologies and protocols to increase the capacity of the network, allowing more devices to connect simultaneously without reducing the quality of the connection.

4G networks are widely available in many countries around the world and are used by millions of people to connect to the Internet and make phone calls on their mobile devices.

Here is an overview of the SIP-I handshake process:

The first device sends a SIP INVITE message to the second device, which contains information about the caller and the desired call parameters (such as the codec to be used for encoding the audio).

The second device responds with a SIP 100 Trying message, which indicates that it has received the INVITE message and is processing the request.

The second device sends a SIP 180 Ringing message, which indicates that the called device is ringing.

If the called device is available and accepts the call, it sends a SIP 200 OK message to the calling device. This message includes information about the call parameters that were negotiated between the two devices.

The calling device responds with an ACK message, which confirms that it has received the 200 OK message.

At this point, the call is established, and the two devices can begin exchanging audio data using the Real-time Transport Protocol (RTP) over ISDN channels.

When one of the devices wants to end the call, it sends a SIP BYE message to the other device. The other device responds with a 200 OK message, and the call is terminated.

This is a basic overview of the SIP-I handshake process. There are many other SIP messages and procedures that can be used in different situations, such as to modify the call parameters or to handle errors.

Question: Explain ISDN Answer: Integrated Services Digital Network (ISDN) is a telecommunications standard that allows the transmission of voice and data over digital telephone lines or normal telephone wires. ISDN is based on the concept of "channels," which are like virtual telephone lines that can be used to carry different types of data. There are two main types of channels in ISDN:

B channels: These channels are used to carry voice and data. A single B channel can handle data at speeds of up to 64 kbps (kilobits per second).

D channels: These channels are used to control the connection between two devices and to transmit signaling information.

ISDN offers several advantages over traditional telephone systems. It allows for faster data transfer rates, better quality of service, and easier connection to the internet. It also enables multiple devices, such as computers and fax machines, to share a single telephone line. However, ISDN has largely been replaced by newer technologies such as broadband and VoIP (Voice over Internet Protocol).

Question: Explain the role of fiber optics in telecommunications Answer: Fiber optics play a significant role in telecommunications by providing a fast and efficient means of transmitting data over long distances.

Fiber optic cables are made of thin strands of glass or plastic, each about the diameter of a human hair. These strands are coated in a reflective material and arranged in a bundle, called a "fiber." Light is used to transmit data through the fibers, and the reflection of the light off the inner walls of the fibers allows the data to be transmitted over long distances without loss of quality.

There are several advantages to using fiber optics in telecommunications. First, fiber optic cables are very thin and lightweight, making them easy to install and maintain. They are also immune to interference from electrical fields, which makes them more reliable than traditional copper cables. In addition, fiber optic cables have a much higher capacity for data transmission, allowing for faster speeds and more efficient communication.

Overall, the use of fiber optics has greatly enhanced the capabilities of telecommunications systems by providing a fast and reliable means of transmitting data over long distances. It has enabled the development of new technologies such as broadband internet, video conferencing, and streaming media, and has played a key role in the globalization of communication.
